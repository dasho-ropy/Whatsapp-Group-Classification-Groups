While it is not untrue that a clean environment is desired by all persons and society at large and that there is need to carry out regular sanitation exercise, most persons will admit the undesirability of the restriction of their movement on a so called environmental sanitation day, more so the undue embarrassment meted on them by law enforcement personnel and agents of the Environmental Sanitation Authority on such days.  The monthly compulsory sanitation exercise of many states in Nigeria takes place on the last Saturday of every month at the early hours within which time persons are compulsorily required to stay at home until 10:00am. Defaulters are arrested, convicted and fined, sometimes after passing through dehumanizing treatment. This was what happened in the case of Faith Okafor v Attorney General of Lagos. In that case the plaintiff appealed her conviction for movement on a designated sanitation day by the Special Offences Court, at the Court of Appeal, Lagos Division. There the court, reversing the conviction, sternly stated inter alia, that an executive directive of the Lagos State Government could not validly derogate the Right to Freedom of Movement of the plaintiff. Clearly the reasoning of the court in that case is clearly justified by the fact that the violation of the plaintiffâ€™s right was done pursuant to a mere executive directive and not a law and therefore contrary to sections 41 & 45 of the Constitution. However, it is the writerâ€™s strong contention in this work that such a limitation of human rights on basis of environmental sanitation, even where imposed by a written law, would be excessive and out of proportion contrary to sections 41 and 45 respectively and therefore unconstitutional, null and void.  The Fundamental Right to Freedom of Movement is constitutionally guaranteed under Section 41(1) which provides that â€œevery citizen of Nigeria is entitled to move freely throughout Nigeria and to reside in any part thereof, and no citizen of Nigeria shall be expelled from Nigeria or refused entry thereto or exit therefromâ€. It simply means every citizen of Nigeria has the right to go to and fro, within, exit and re-enter Nigeria. It means liberty to move about at his will. However under subsection 2 of section 41 the right could be derogated by law. Thus subsection 2(a) justifies for example the seizure of the passport of a criminal suspect, or convict, or his incarceration although within the scope of the law. Subsection 2(b) relates to extradition of a criminal or criminal suspect to another country. Further, under the blanket provision of section 45 the right could be derogated by a law in the interest of defence, public safety, public order, public morality or public health, or for the purpose of protecting the rights and freedom of other persons. This provision justifies, inter alia, restrictions on freedom of movement, and thus personal liberty of criminals and suspects, in terms of imprisonment or detention and preventive detention as permitted under section 35 of the Constitution. It can also be used to justify curfew, and the restriction of citizens to enemy countries. Undoubtedly authors and enforcers of such limitation would readily claim justification from section 45, particularly on grounds of public health; but the question that the courts must answer is whether such law is reasonably justifiable in a democratic society. What does this mean?  Indeed there is no perfect template for a democratic society; consequently in a democratic setting it is the courts that determine whether or not a law is reasonably justifiable by way of judicial review. Thus, to quote Justice Bates in Cheranci v Cheranci [1960] NRNLR 24; a reasonably justifiable restriction on human rights â€œmust not be excessive or out of proportion to the objects which it is sought to achieve.â€ This is akin to the UK where a common way of determining whether a law that limits rights is reasonably justifiable in a democratic society is by determining whether the law is proportionate.
